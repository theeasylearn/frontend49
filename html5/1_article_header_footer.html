15 places to visit in india for foreigners

Varansi

Varanasi, one of the world’s oldest continuously inhabited cities, stands as a living symbol of India’s spiritual and cultural soul. Located along the sacred Ganges River in Uttar Pradesh, Varanasi attracts travelers, pilgrims, and seekers from across the globe.

Varanasi, often regarded as India’s spiritual epicenter, is a captivating city where ancient traditions and vibrant life flow seamlessly along the banks of the sacred Ganges River. Pilgrims flock here from across India and around the globe, drawn by the promise of moksha—liberation from the cycle of rebirth—which is believed to be attained by dying in this holy city or having one’s ashes absorbed by the Ganges. The city’s more than 80 ghats, especially Dashashwamedh and Manikarnika Ghat, are the beating heart of daily rituals, from sunrise puja ceremonies and communal bathing to the unforgettable spectacle of the evening Ganga Aarti, where priests perform synchronized offerings of fire and music to honor the river goddess.

Wandering through Varanasi’s narrow, labyrinthine lanes reveals a sensory tapestry—ancient temples such as the iconic Kashi Vishwanath, bustling markets famous for Banarasi silk and brassware, and aromatic street food stalls. The city’s energetic yet contemplative atmosphere draws spiritual seekers, photographers, and travelers eager to witness its profound rituals and centuries-old customs. Beyond the city, the serene site of Sarnath offers Buddhist heritage and quiet reflection. Varanasi is not just a destination; it is a place that awakens a deeper understanding of life, death, and the enduring rhythms of Indian culture.

A visit to Varanasi is much more than a journey to a historic city; it is an immersion into the heart of India’s spirituality and timeless traditions. Here, life and death are inextricably intertwined, and every corner tells a story of faith, culture, and resilience.

Somnath Temple

The Somnath Temple, located on the western coast of Gujarat, stands as a resplendent testament to faith, resilience, and architectural grandeur. Revered as one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva, Somnath is both a sacred pilgrimage site and a symbol of India’s enduring spiritual heritage.

The history of Somnath Temple stretches back thousands of years, with legends intertwining devotion and mythology. Over centuries, it has been destroyed and rebuilt several times, each reconstruction reflecting the unbreakable spirit of the devotees. The current structure, rising majestically above the Arabian Sea, showcases exquisite Chalukya-style architecture, adorned with intricate carvings and soaring spires. As waves crash against the temple’s foundation, the chants of devotees create a mesmerizing blend of nature and spirituality.

Visitors are drawn not only to the temple’s spiritual aura but also to its stunning coastal views and the stories etched into its stones. The evening aarti, held against the backdrop of the setting sun, is an unforgettable spectacle of lights, music, and devotion. Museums and nearby sites such as Triveni Sangam add cultural and historical layers to the experience. Somnath is more than a pilgrimage—it is a journey through India’s resilient past and living faith.

A visit to Somnath Temple is both a spiritual and cultural revelation, offering peace, inspiration, and a profound sense of connection to India's ancient legacy. The temple’s steadfast presence is a reminder of the enduring power of faith and the timeless beauty of devotion.
