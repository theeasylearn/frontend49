The Haunted Library

In a quiet town, there was an old library that had been abandoned for years. One evening, a young man named Aryan, who was an avid reader, decided to explore the library. As he stepped inside, the musty smell of old books filled the air, and the dim light flickered. Aryan began searching for rare books, unaware of the presence lurking in the shadows. The clock struck midnight, and the eerie silence was broken by soft whispers. Aryan turned, but there was no one in sight. The whispers grew louder, and suddenly, the bookshelves began to rattle. As Aryan moved closer to one shelf, a cold hand touched his shoulder. He spun around, but no one was there. Terrified, Aryan tried to leave, but the door was locked. The whispers now turned into a deafening scream. A ghostly figure appeared before him, the spirit of a librarian who had died mysteriously in the library. The figure reached out, and Aryan felt an icy grip. The last thing he saw was the library's books, their pages flipping as if they were alive. The next morning, the library was quiet again, but Aryan was never seen again.

The Birth of Lord Ganesha

Once upon a time, Goddess Parvati was preparing for a bath in her palace. She created a statue of a boy out of clay and breathed life into it. She named him Ganesha and asked him to guard the door while she bathed. When Lord Shiva, her husband, arrived, Ganesha, not knowing who he was, stopped him from entering. Shiva, enraged by being denied entry, fought with Ganesha. In the heat of the moment, Shiva beheaded Ganesha. Parvati, devastated by the loss of her son, pleaded with Shiva to restore him. Moved by her sorrow, Shiva ordered his followers to bring the head of the first living being they encountered. They returned with the head of an elephant, which Shiva attached to Ganesha’s body, bringing him back to life. Thus, Ganesha was born again, and he became the remover of obstacles, symbolizing wisdom, new beginnings, and the triumph of good over evil. Every year, people celebrate his birth during the festival of Ganesh Chaturthi.

The Mirror’s Curse

In an old mansion on the outskirts of the city, there was a cursed mirror. It was said that anyone who gazed into it would see their darkest fears come alive. One evening, a woman named Priya moved into the mansion, unaware of its sinister history. Priya was cleaning the attic when she came across the mirror, covered in dust. Intrigued, she wiped the surface clean, and as she looked into it, her reflection smiled back at her — but Priya did not smile. Suddenly, the reflection’s face twisted into a grotesque expression, and it began to speak. “You cannot escape me,” it whispered, as the room grew colder. Priya tried to break the mirror, but every time she turned away, the reflection mimicked her actions, only it was always a step ahead. The reflection began to move outside the mirror, and Priya realized it was no longer just a reflection. The room filled with the sound of laughing, and before she knew it, she was surrounded by her own fears. Priya was never seen again, but the mirror still stands, waiting for the next curious soul to gaze into it.

The Story of Lord Rama’s Exile

Lord Rama, the seventh avatar of Lord Vishnu, was the beloved prince of Ayodhya. His father, King Dasharatha, decided to crown Rama as the next king. However, due to a promise made to his second wife, Kaikeyi, Dasharatha was forced to send Rama into exile for fourteen years. Despite the injustice, Rama obeyed his father's command without hesitation. His wife, Sita, and his loyal brother, Lakshmana, chose to accompany him into the forest. The trio faced many hardships during their exile, but Rama remained steadfast in his duty to uphold righteousness (dharma). During this period, the demon king Ravana abducted Sita, leading to a great war. With the help of Hanuman and an army of monkeys, Rama fought and defeated Ravana, rescuing Sita. Rama’s return to Ayodhya after his exile marked the celebration of Diwali, the festival of lights, symbolizing the victory of good over evil.
